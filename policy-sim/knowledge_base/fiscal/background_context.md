# UK Fiscal Policy Background — 2010 Period

## Macroeconomic Setting

The UK entered 2010 emerging from the 2008/09 recession. GDP contracted by approximately 4.2% in 2009, the steepest post-war downturn at that point. The fiscal deficit reached approximately 10% of GDP in 2009/10 — roughly £155 billion — driven by recession-related revenue collapse and the 2008 bank rescue operations.

The Coalition Government (Conservative-Liberal Democrat) took office in May 2010 and made fiscal consolidation its central economic objective. The June 2010 Emergency Budget initiated a programme combining spending cuts and tax rises. The OBR projected the structural deficit would be eliminated by 2014/15.

## VAT and Household Spending Patterns

UK VAT applies to roughly 50–60% of household final consumption expenditure. The remainder falls into zero-rated or exempt categories. Key spending categories by income group relevance:

| Category | VAT Rate | Lower quintile relevance | Upper quintile relevance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Food (most) | 0% | High | Moderate |
| Restaurant/takeaway | 20% | Moderate | Higher |
| Domestic energy | 5% | High (high share of income) | Moderate |
| Clothing (adult) | 20% | Moderate | Higher |
| Children's clothing | 0% | High for families | N/A |
| Private transport/fuel | 20% + fuel duty | High | High |
| Household goods | 20% | Moderate | Higher |
| Leisure/entertainment | 20% | Lower | Higher |

The combination of zero-rated food and reduced-rate energy moderates but does not eliminate the regressive income-share effect. Because lower-income households save less, a higher fraction of their total income is exposed to consumption taxes.

## Policy Debate Context (2010–2011)

The VAT rise generated distributional debate:

**Government position:** Revenue-raising component of a broadly-shared consolidation; necessary for deficit reduction; benefit uprating would partially offset impact on low-income households; broadly proportional as share of expenditure.

**Opposition and IFS:** Regressive as a share of income; disproportionate impact on pensioners and low-income working families who cannot reduce consumption or increase savings; compounded by simultaneous benefit and public service cuts affecting the same groups.

**Business community:** Consumer demand suppression concern; retailers and hospitality most exposed; VAT-registered businesses can reclaim input VAT so direct business-side cost is limited; main risk is revenue reduction from lower customer spending.
