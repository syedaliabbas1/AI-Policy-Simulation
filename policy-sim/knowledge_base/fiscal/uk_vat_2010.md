# UK Value Added Tax Rise, January 2011

## Policy Summary

On 22 June 2010, Chancellor George Osborne announced in the Emergency Budget that the standard rate of Value Added Tax (VAT) would rise from 17.5% to 20%. The increase took effect on 4 January 2011. The change was a 2.5 percentage point rise on the pre-crisis rate, expected to raise approximately £13 billion annually by 2014/15.

## Context

The VAT rise was the centrepiece revenue measure in the Coalition Government's fiscal consolidation programme following the 2008 financial crisis. The OBR forecast the UK deficit at approximately 10.1% of GDP in 2010/11. The government's stated objective was to eliminate the structural deficit over a parliament through a combination of spending cuts (approximately 80% of consolidation) and tax rises (approximately 20%).

VAT had previously been temporarily reduced from 17.5% to 15% in December 2008 as a counter-cyclical stimulus measure, returning to 17.5% in January 2010. The 2011 rise to 20% therefore exceeded the pre-crisis baseline.

## Structure of UK VAT

UK VAT applies at the standard rate to most goods and services. Key exceptions:

- **Zero-rated (0%):** Most food, children's clothing, books and newspapers, most prescription medicines, water supply for households
- **Reduced rate (5%):** Domestic fuel and power, children's car seats, certain energy-saving home improvements
- **Exempt:** Financial services, insurance, most healthcare, most education, postal services

The zero-rating of most food is the most significant exception for distributional analysis. However, restaurant and takeaway meals, alcoholic drinks, crisps, confectionery, and ice cream are standard-rated.

## Distributional Context

Analysis by the Institute for Fiscal Studies (IFS) examined the distributional effects of the 2010–2014 fiscal consolidation package. Key findings for the VAT component:

- The VAT rise was broadly proportional as a share of household **expenditure** — households at different income levels spend similar proportions of their expenditure on standard-rated goods
- As a share of **net household income**, the rise was regressive — lower-income households have lower savings rates and therefore spend a higher fraction of their income on VAT-applicable consumption
- The bottom income quintile faced an estimated loss of approximately 1.7–1.9% of net income from the VAT component
- The top income quintile faced an estimated loss of approximately 0.5–0.7% of net income

The key mechanism: savings are not subject to VAT, so households that save a larger fraction of income (typically higher-income households) face a lower effective VAT burden as a proportion of income. Pensioners and low-income workers with minimal savings are proportionally most exposed.

## Impact on Specific Population Groups

**Low-income working households:** High exposure due to high consumption-to-income ratio and limited zero-rated spending (they still spend on standard-rated clothing, transport, household goods). Benefits uprating offers partial but not full offset.

**VAT-registered businesses:** Can reclaim VAT paid on business inputs. Direct business cost limited. Main concern is demand-side: consumer purchasing power reduces, potentially suppressing orders.

**Retired households on fixed incomes:** High essential spend share (heating at 5%, food at 0%, but clothing, household goods, transport at 20%). No ability to increase income. Heating cost increase particularly salient (5% not affected, but fuel-related products are).

**Higher-income households:** Proportionally lower exposure as a share of income due to higher savings rates. Absolute cash impact may be higher, but relative burden lower.
